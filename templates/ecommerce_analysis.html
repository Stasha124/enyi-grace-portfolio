Customer Behaviour, Funnel Performance & Revenue Insights (2020‚Äì2025)

This case study presents a detailed marketing analytics project based on a multi-year E-Commerce dataset covering the period from 2020 to 2025. The analysis combines transactional data, clickstream events, customer profiles and reviews to understand how customers browse, purchase and provide feedback, and how these behaviours translate into revenue and long-term value for the business.

The underlying database integrates orders, order items, products, customers, events/clickstream, and product reviews. This enables both a financial view (revenue, profit, margins) and a behavioural view (add-to-cart, checkout, churn risk, rating patterns). Each section below summarises a key part of the analysis, supported by visualisations and interpreted through a marketing lens. For every topic, the report presents a clear insight and a concrete recommendation for decision-making.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Yearly Revenue, Profit and Order Behaviour (2020‚Äì2025)

The first stage of the analysis focuses on overall financial performance. Transactions are grouped by order_year and summarised using key indicators: total revenue, total profit, average order value (AOV), average profit margin, and order counts. This provides a clean snapshot of annual business health and growth.

Yearly revenue fluctuates within a relatively narrow range of approximately $1.47M to $1.76M and profit margins remain stable around 27‚Äì28%. Average order value stays between $166 and $173. There are modest dips in some years (such as 2022 and 2025), but no structural collapse in demand. Overall, the trend reflects a mature and resilient E-Commerce operation with predictable customer spend patterns.

üß† Insight

Stable revenue, profit and AOV indicate a strong product‚Äìmarket fit and a customer base that consistently engages with the store. However, the small downward variations in revenue show that the business is not fully maximising growth opportunities in certain years or seasons.

The company enjoys a solid baseline but would benefit from targeted campaigns designed to turn flat or marginally negative years into periods of deliberate growth.

üéØ Recommendation

Implement a structured annual marketing calendar aligned with peak demand moments (e.g. New Year health campaigns, back-to-school, summer promotions, Black Friday/Cyber Monday). For each year:

  • Identify historically weaker months and attach targeted promotions to boost performance.
  • Plan category-focused campaigns around high-margin or strategic products.
  • Monitor year-on-year revenue changes during campaign windows to quantify impact.

This transforms a stable business into a strategically managed growth engine.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Country-Level Revenue and Profitability

To understand geographic performance, the analysis aggregates total revenue, total profit, average order value, and average profit margin by order_country. This comparison highlights which markets drive the largest share of turnover and which deliver the strongest economics per order.

Some countries operate as high-volume, high-revenue markets, while others generate smaller sales volumes but stronger margins. This mix reflects differences in competition, pricing, product availability and local customer expectations.

üß† Insight

The business benefits from both mature high-volume markets and smaller high-margin markets. High-volume markets provide reliability and scale, whereas high-margin markets offer attractive unit profitability and expansion potential.

A one-size-fits-all marketing approach would fail to exploit these distinct strengths.

üéØ Recommendation

Adopt a tiered geo-strategy:

  • In Tier 1 (high-volume) markets, focus on retention, loyalty and subscription initiatives to consolidate market share.
  • In Tier 2 (high-margin) markets, invest in acquisition: localised creatives, language support, partnerships with local influencers and region-specific campaigns.
  • Track CAC and LTV per region to continuously refine budget allocation.

This ensures marketing spend is aligned with both scale and profitability.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Customer Segmentation and Marketing Opt-In Behaviour

The customer and transactions tables are merged to build a customer-level dataset. For each customer, the analysis calculates total revenue, total orders and average order value (AOV), together with demographic attributes and marketing_opt_in status.

At a global level, average order value is compared between customers who opted in to marketing and those who opted out. The bar chart shows that both segments maintain a relatively high AOV, with only small differences between them.

üß† Insight

Opt-in status alone does not radically alter the value of a single order. However, this metric does not capture frequency or lifetime spend. Subsequent analysis shows that opt-in customers typically place more orders and therefore generate higher total revenue over time.

AOV is important, but it is the combination of AOV and order frequency that defines true customer value.

The second chart breaks down AOV by age band and opt-in status. It reveals that some age groups, particularly the 35‚Äì44 and 65+ segments, tend to have noticeably higher average order values. In some age groups, opt-in customers spend slightly more; in others, opt-out customers lead.

üß† Insight

Customer value is not uniform across age bands. Middle-aged and older customers often have more disposable income, which translates into higher average baskets. Marketing opt-in behaviour interacts with age, suggesting that some demographics are more receptive to ongoing communication than others.

This provides a basis for age-aware segmentation in campaign planning.

To simplify customer value differences, the analysis groups customers into Low-Value, Mid-Value and High-Value segments based on total revenue. Within each group, the proportion of customers who opted in to marketing is compared to those who did not.

Across all segments, there are more opt-in customers than opt-out customers, and this pattern persists even at the high-value end. This confirms that marketing subscribers are overrepresented among the most commercially valuable customers.

üß† Insight

Marketing opt-in is a strong indicator of engagement and long-term value. High-value customers are more likely to be reachable through email or other direct channels, which further amplifies their importance to the business.

Ignoring the opt-in segment would mean under-utilising the most responsive subset of the customer base.

üéØ Recommendation

Use this segmentation to build a layered CRM strategy:

  • Develop high-value subscriber journeys (VIP content, early access, tailored offers) for high-value opt-in customers.
  • Create activation campaigns to encourage mid-value and low-value customers to opt in and increase frequency.
  • Adjust messaging and channel mix by age band to reflect different preferences and price sensitivities.

This approach aligns marketing resources with the customers most likely to drive long-term growth.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Customer Inactivity and Churn Risk

To assess churn risk, the analysis calculates each customer‚Äôs last order date and measures how many days have passed since that purchase. Customers are placed into inactivity bands: Active (<1 month), 1‚Äì3 months, 3‚Äì6 months, 6‚Äì12 months, and Dormant (>1 year).

The majority of customers fall into the 6‚Äì12 month and >1 year categories, indicating large pools of customers who have lapsed without re-engagement. Only a small proportion are currently active.

üß† Insight

The business faces significant latent churn but also possesses a substantial reactivation opportunity. Inactive customers are familiar with the brand, so re-engaging them is generally cheaper than acquiring entirely new customers.

Without structured lifecycle marketing, this potential revenue is left on the table.

üéØ Recommendation

Implement an automated win-back programme triggered by inactivity thresholds:

  • At 60‚Äì90 days: reminder campaigns with personalised product recommendations.
  • At 6‚Äì12 months: stronger incentives (discounts, bundles, free shipping) to re-start purchasing.
  • At >1 year: ‚ÄúWe want you back‚Äù messaging plus short surveys to identify churn drivers.

Tracking reactivation rate and incremental revenue allows the business to quantify retention ROI.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Top Products by Revenue and Profit

At the product level, the analysis aggregates total revenue, total profit, average profit margin, and number of orders per SKU. A small number of products generate a disproportionate share of revenue and profit, functioning as hero products within the catalogue.

Many of these top products also deliver healthy margins, confirming that they are both popular and profitable. They likely act as key entry points for new customers and as repeat purchase drivers for existing ones.

üß† Insight

The sales pattern follows a classic 80/20 rule, where a minority of products account for the majority of revenue. These SKUs are central to brand identity and should be treated as strategic marketing assets.

Under-leveraging these hero products in promotional activity would limit revenue potential.

üéØ Recommendation

Place hero products at the heart of merchandising and marketing:

  • Feature them in hero banners, recommendation carousels and category landing pages.
  • Use them in bundle offers with complementary products to raise AOV.
  • Highlight strong ratings and reviews on these SKUs as social proof.

At the same time, monitor low-performing SKUs for improvement, repositioning or retirement.

6Ô∏è‚É£ Top Customers: Revenue and Order Frequency

Customers are ranked by total revenue, and their number of orders is examined alongside this ranking. Some top customers reach high revenue through a smaller number of large purchases, while others place many more orders at slightly lower values.

This highlights two archetypes: high-ticket customers and high-frequency customers. Both contribute significantly to revenue but are likely motivated by different things and therefore require different loyalty strategies.

üß† Insight

A uniform approach to all high-value customers would ignore important behavioural differences. Some respond best to exclusivity, others to ongoing rewards and convenience. Differentiated treatment maximises the perceived value of the relationship.

Recognising these profiles enables more effective, targeted loyalty design.

üéØ Recommendation

Build tiered loyalty journeys:

  • Offer priority access, exclusive launches and personalised account management to high-ticket customers.
  • Offer points, streak rewards and repeat purchase incentives to high-frequency customers.
  • Use RFM (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) scoring to keep these segments dynamically updated over time.

This creates a loyalty system that feels tailored and builds deeper emotional connection with top customers.

7Ô∏è‚É£ Clickstream Funnel: Add-to-Cart vs Checkout

Clickstream events capture behavioural interactions such as page_view, add_to_cart and checkout. By counting these events per year, the analysis measures how efficiently purchase intent (adding to cart) is translated into actual checkout attempts.

Most traffic goes to product pages, but there is a big drop from page views ‚Üí add to cart ‚Üí checkout. Checkout friction and product-page hesitation are the main weaknesses. Purchase events are missing product IDs, showing a tracking issue. Fixing tracking, improving product pages, and simplifying checkout will significantly increase conversions and revenue.

üß† Insight

The store is successful at generating product interest but loses a significant portion of potential orders between basket and checkout. Typical causes include unexpected fees, lack of payment choice, complex forms or insufficient reassurance about delivery and returns.

Improving this final stage of the funnel can increase revenue without increasing traffic or ad spend.

üéØ Recommendation

Optimise both the checkout UX and recovery flows:

  • Simplify forms, reduce mandatory fields and offer guest checkout.
  • Display shipping cost and estimated delivery clearly before payment.
  • Support multiple payment methods, including local and wallet options.
  • Trigger abandoned cart reminders via email or notifications within 1‚Äì24 hours, featuring the exact items left behind.

Even modest improvements in cart-to-checkout conversion can translate into substantial incremental revenue.

8Ô∏è‚É£ Customer Review Ratings Distribution

The reviews dataset records 1‚Äì5‚òÖ ratings for products. Analysing the frequency of each rating reveals a distribution heavily skewed towards 4‚òÖ and 5‚òÖ reviews, with relatively few 1‚òÖ and 2‚òÖ ratings and a moderate share of 3‚òÖ ratings.

This pattern implies that most customers are satisfied or very satisfied with their purchases. The small minority of negative reviews tend to focus on specific issues such as product defects, shipping delays or mismatched expectations.

üß† Insight

The skew towards positive ratings confirms that the store delivers a consistently strong experience. This is a crucial foundation for brand trust and reduces perceived risk for new customers.

At the same time, negative reviews provide highly targeted feedback that can guide operational improvements.

üéØ Recommendation

Use ratings as both a conversion tool and a quality signal:

  • Showcase strong average ratings and prominent review counts on product pages and category listings.
  • Incorporate review snippets into email campaigns and paid media creatives.
  • Monitor low ratings and respond quickly with customer service and, where appropriate, compensation.

This reinforces confidence for prospective buyers and helps retain existing customers.

9Ô∏è‚É£ Customer Rating Trends and Sentiment Over Time

Monthly average ratings are plotted across the entire period, together with a 5-month rolling average. Individual months fluctuate around the long-term mean, but the rolling line smooths these variations and reveals the underlying trend.

The smoothed curve shows that average ratings remain consistently high, with only mild dips that are later corrected. This demonstrates robust, sustained customer satisfaction over time rather than temporary spikes.

A yearly view of average ratings confirms that, despite small year-to-year changes, ratings stay within a narrow band close to 4 out of 5. There is no evidence of long-term decline.

This stability suggests that the business is maintaining product quality and service standards even as it grows and changes across the six-year period.

To complement numeric ratings, sentiment scores are derived from the text of reviews and plotted alongside average ratings. The sentiment curve broadly follows the same movements as the rating curve ‚Äì when ratings dip slightly, sentiment also becomes more neutral; when ratings rise, sentiment becomes more positive.

üß† Insight

The combination of monthly, yearly and sentiment trends shows that customer satisfaction is both high and stable. Sentiment analysis validates numeric ratings and can act as an early warning system when subtle changes occur.

Occasional dips likely correspond to specific operational or product issues, rather than a systemic decline in experience.

üéØ Recommendation

Integrate rating and sentiment monitoring into routine business dashboards:

  • Track monthly rating and sentiment by product category and geography.
  • Investigate sharp deviations promptly, linking them to changes in supply, pricing or service.
  • Highlight consistently high-rated categories in marketing as ‚Äúcustomer-loved‚Äù ranges.

This closes the loop between customer feedback and ongoing service improvement.

üîü Product Popularity: Ratings vs Number of Reviews

Finally, average rating is plotted against number of reviews for each product. Products with more reviews represent higher visibility and engagement. Most products cluster between approximately 3.8 and 4.5‚òÖ, and many high-review products retain strong ratings.

Popular items therefore remain well-regarded even under heavy scrutiny, while some lesser-reviewed products may be high quality but lack sufficient social proof to attract hesitant buyers.

üß† Insight

Reviews play a dual role: they reflect popularity and actively contribute to it. High-review, high-rating products form a set of trusted best-sellers, whereas low-review products require deliberate support to gain momentum.

Combining rating level with review count allows the business to prioritise products that are both reliable and influential in the catalogue.

üéØ Recommendation

Use the relationship between ratings and review volume to guide merchandising decisions:

  • Label high-rating, high-review products as ‚ÄúTop Rated‚Äù or ‚ÄúMost Loved‚Äù.
  • Promote low-review but high-rating products as ‚ÄúHidden Gems‚Äù to stimulate discovery.
  • Encourage early buyers of new items to leave reviews via follow-up emails and in-app prompts.

This strengthens the review flywheel and builds trust across the entire product range.

üìà Overall Conclusion

This E-Commerce marketing analytics project demonstrates how integrated data from transactions, clickstream behaviour and customer reviews can be transformed into clear, actionable insights. The business exhibits strong fundamentals: stable revenue and margins, high overall satisfaction, valuable hero products and a core of loyal, high-value customers.

At the same time, the analysis reveals significant opportunities:

  • Retention: large pools of inactive customers can be reactivated through structured lifecycle campaigns.
  • Funnel optimisation: improving cart-to-checkout conversion offers immediate revenue upside.
  • Personalisation: marketing opt-in and behavioural segments support tailored CRM journeys.
  • Geo and product focus: targeted emphasis on high-margin countries and hero products amplifies ROI.
  • Feedback-driven decisions: ratings and sentiment trends provide continuous input for product and service improvement.

Overall, this case study illustrates how data analysis can move beyond descriptive reporting to inform strategic marketing decisions. It connects customer behaviour with practical growth strategies in a real-world E-Commerce context.
